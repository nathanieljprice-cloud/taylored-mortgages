Spring has arrived in Maine, and so has a familiar question from buyers across my pipeline: "Is now a good time to buy, or should I wait?"

My honest answer hasn't changed much from last year — but the reasons behind it have shifted. Here's what I'm seeing on the ground in May 2026.

## Inventory Is Up, But Don't Get Too Excited

Statewide inventory has climbed about 18% compared to this time last year, which sounds encouraging. The reality is more nuanced. In Portland's most desirable neighborhoods — the West End, Deering, and the Eastern Promenade corridor — active listings are still running below 1.5 months of supply. A balanced market is typically 4–6 months. We're nowhere close.

The South Portland and Scarborough markets tell a similar story. Homes priced correctly are still moving in under two weeks, and multiple-offer situations are common for anything in the $400K–$575K range.

## Where I'm Actually Seeing Opportunity

The bright spots are the markets that don't make the headlines. Westbrook, Gorham, and parts of Biddeford have seen meaningful inventory increases, with average days-on-market stretching past 25 days in some pockets. That gives buyers room to negotiate — something that felt impossible 18 months ago.

The Midcoast — Bath, Rockland, and the Boothbay area — is also showing more balance, particularly for properties above $650K where the buyer pool thins out naturally.

## Rates: Where We Stand

I'm locking 30-year conventional loans in the 6.625%–6.875% range as of early May. The Fed held rates steady at their last meeting, and the market is currently pricing in one cut before year-end — though that could shift with any significant economic data.

The more important number for most of my buyers is the monthly payment, not the rate itself. A 6.75% rate on a $450,000 loan is $2,919/month — workable for many households, and refinanceable when rates drop.

## My Advice for Spring Buyers

If you've been pre-approved and you find a home that checks your boxes, don't let the rate be the reason you walk away. The appreciation you'd miss waiting for a rate drop often outweighs the monthly savings — especially in markets where prices are still moving up 3–4% annually.
